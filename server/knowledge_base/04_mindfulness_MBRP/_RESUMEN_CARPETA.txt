```{=html}
<p>
```
Objectives: This systematic review aimed to understand the current state
of the art about the effectiveness of mindfulness-based relapse
prevention (MBRP) on individuals with substance use disorders (SUD),
taking into account not only SUD variables (e.g., cravings, frequency of
use) but also other relevant clinical variables (e.g., anxiety and
depressive symptoms, quality of life). Methods: A comprehensive search
of electronic databases was conducted to identify studies that
investigate MBRP interventions on individuals with SUD. Studies that met
the inclusion criteria were synthesized and assessed using systematic
review methods. Results: Thirteen studies were included in the present
review. The methodological quality of the included studies was
moderately strong. Nine studies (69.2%) used the traditional 16 h MBRP
program. Six studies (46.1%) chose to use a co-intervention treatment
ranging from the treatment as usual (TAU) to cognitive behavioral
therapy. All but one study indicated that their interventions produced
positive effects on at least one addiction outcome measure. None of the
interventions were evaluated across different settings or populations.
Conclusions: Despite some heterogeneity regarding the type of MBRP
program used, results support the effectiveness of these interventions
in the SUD population, especially in reducing cravings, decreasing the
frequency of use, and improving depressive symptoms.

```{=html}
<title>
```
1.  Introduction
    ```{=html}
    </title>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Substance use disorders (SUD) affect the lives of more than 35
    million people worldwide according to 2019 data from the United
    Nations Office on Drugs and Crime
    \[`<xref rid="B1-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}1`</xref>`{=html}\].
    A chronic disease, substance addiction is associated with
    significant costs not only for the individual but for society at
    large. It often starts with occasional substance use in a
    recreational context, followed by a progressive increase in
    frequency of use as the desired effects are diminished by
    habituation
    \[`<xref rid="B2-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}2`</xref>`{=html}\].
    This process is mediated by genetic, developmental, and psychosocial
    factors that impact an individual's relative vulnerability to the
    development of an addiction
    \[`<xref rid="B3-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}3`</xref>`{=html}\].
    Although there are many different types of SUD, depending on the
    main substance of choice---with DSM-V offering diagnoses for the
    abuse of alcohol, cannabis, hallucinogens, opioids, and even
    caffeine, among others
    \[`<xref rid="B4-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}4`</xref>`{=html}\]---there
    are underlying mechanisms in addiction that appear to be common to
    all substances
    \[`<xref rid="B3-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}3`</xref>`{=html}\].
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Given the prevalence and manifest individual and socioeconomic
    consequences of substance addiction, there has been a considerable
    effort by the scientific community to create and study effective
    treatments for SUD
    \[`<xref rid="B5-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}5`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B6-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}6`</xref>`{=html}\].
    Despite this, relapse rates remain incredibly high, with data
    suggesting that more than two thirds of individuals return to
    substance use within a year of treatment
    \[`<xref rid="B7-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}7`</xref>`{=html}\].
    The relapsing nature of substance addiction has made it clear that
    relapse prevention is an essential treatment component for long-term
    recovery, shifting attention to interventions that propose to
    address this need.
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    The most common intervention targeting relapse prevention is based
    on the identification of idiosyncratic high-risk situations that can
    increase the probability of relapse and the development of specific
    strategies that the individual can resort to when exposed to them
    \[`<xref rid="B8-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}8`</xref>`{=html}\].
    However, recently, other types of interventions aiming to improve
    the effectiveness of SUD treatment and address relapse prevention
    have been emerging, as is the case with mindfulness-based
    interventions
    \[`<xref rid="B9-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}9`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B10-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}10`</xref>`{=html}\].
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Mindfulness is a mental state that involves an awareness of the
    present moment and a non-judgmental posture towards internal and
    external phenomena. Practicing mindfulness has been shown to help
    regulate attention, cultivate acceptance, and develop the ability to
    observe inner experiences (thoughts, feelings, and physical
    sensations) without judgment
    \[`<xref rid="B11-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}11`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B12-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}12`</xref>`{=html}\].
    In the context of addiction treatment, it can promote awareness of
    external and internal triggers for addictive behaviors and improve
    tolerance to uncomfortable emotional, cognitive, and physical
    experiences
    \[`<xref rid="B13-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}13`</xref>`{=html}\].
    It can also have a significant effect on decreasing cravings after
    treatment
    \[`<xref rid="B14-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}14`</xref>`{=html}\].
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP) is an intervention that
    integrates mindfulness meditation with traditional relapse
    prevention techniques. It has three main components: formal
    mindfulness practice, informal practice, and coping strategies. The
    formal practice includes body scan, sitting meditation (breath,
    body, thoughts, and emotions), mountain meditation, and
    loving-kindness meditation. The informal practice includes urge
    surfing, mindfulness of daily activities, SOBER breathing space, and
    mindful movement
    \[`<xref rid="B15-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}15`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B16-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}16`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B17-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}17`</xref>`{=html}\].
    Some of the informal practices can be used as coping strategies due
    to the fact that they can help the patients cope with difficulties,
    emotional challenges, or stress
    \[`<xref rid="B18-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}18`</xref>`{=html}\].
    As such, this last component intends to adapt informal practice into
    practical strategies the individuals can use in real-life
    situations.
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    The original MBRP program has a total of 16 h divided into 8 weekly
    sessions (2 h each) but can be adapted according to the target
    population and the researchers' goals
    \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html}\].
    The sessions include mindfulness practices and exercises that aim to
    increase the individual's awareness of internal (emotional and
    cognitive) and external (situational) cues related to substance use
    and help in the development of appropriate coping strategies
    \[`<xref rid="B21-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}21`</xref>`{=html}\].
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    The first three sessions of the program focus on raising the
    individual's awareness of environmental triggers and reactions that
    may occur in response to them. The first session centers on the
    individual's habits, which occur in autopilot mode; the second
    centers on increasing the awareness of triggers and cravings; and
    the third centers on the promotion of mindfulness practice in daily
    life. Sessions four, five, and six focus on mindfulness in the
    context of relapse prevention. The fourth session centers on the use
    of mindfulness in high-risk situations; the fifth session intends to
    teach acceptance and skillful action; and the sixth session centers
    on the concept of defusion, that is, the ability to see thoughts
    only as thoughts and not as reflections of reality. Finally, the
    last two sessions focus on social and environmental factors and have
    the goal of guiding the application of what was learned to everyday
    life
    \[`<xref rid="B15-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}15`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html}\].
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    The aim of this review was to evaluate the effectiveness of MBRP in
    individuals with SUD. Although the search conducted on the Web of
    Science and Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews revealed the
    existence of a systematic review/meta-analysis looking into this
    same question
    \[`<xref rid="B23-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}23`</xref>`{=html}\],
    we considered that the interest this topic has gathered in recent
    years justified another review. It is not only likely but probable
    that in the four years that have passed since Grant et
    al. \[`<xref rid="B23-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}23`</xref>`{=html}\]
    conducted their review, new relevant studies have been added to the
    literature. Given the rapid growth of knowledge concerning this type
    of intervention, we also hope that more recent studies have taken
    into account some of the limitations found in their earlier
    counterparts.
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    To better understand the effectiveness of MBRP in this population,
    we considered the following
    questions:`<list list-type="simple">`{=html}`<list-item>`{=html}`<label>`{=html}I`</label>`{=html}
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Do MBRP interventions lead to significant improvements in SUD
    variables (e.g., cravings, frequency of use) in individuals with
    SUD?
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    `</list-item>`{=html}`<list-item>`{=html}`<label>`{=html}II`</label>`{=html}
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    Do MBRP interventions lead to significant improvements in other
    relevant clinical variables (e.g., anxiety, depression, quality of
    life) in individuals with SUD?
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    `</list-item>`{=html}`<list-item>`{=html}`<label>`{=html}III`</label>`{=html}
    ```{=html}
    <p>
    ```
    How do MBRP interventions compare to other evidence-based
    interventions for individuals with SUD?
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    `</list-item>`{=html}`</list>`{=html}
    ```{=html}
    </p>
    ```
    `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec2-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}

    ```{=html}
    <title>
    ```
    2.  Materials and Methods
        ```{=html}
        </title>
        ```
        `<sec id="sec2dot1-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
        ```{=html}
        <title>
        ```
        2.1. Research Strategy
        ```{=html}
        </title>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        This systematic review was not pre-registered, and the results
        should be considered exploratory.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        Through the research performed on the Cochrane Central Register
        of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), JBI Database of Systematic
        Reviews and Implementation Reports, Database of Promoting Health
        Effectiveness Reviews (DoPHER), Evidence for Policy and Practice
        Information and Coordinating Center (EPPI-Center), and PROSPERO
        International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews, no
        systematic literature review was found after the year 2017.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        Initially, a general search was carried out in Cochrane, Pubmed,
        and Web of Science, after which the terms used in the title and
        abstract of the articles were examined. In a second phase, using
        the identified keywords and descriptors, research was conducted
        on all databases included in this review
        (`<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A1" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
        A1`</xref>`{=html}). Due to the multiplicity of concepts to
        describe the term (addiction) we used a global MeSH term in our
        research. In the last phase of the research, we used the
        bibliography of the selected studies to search for
        additional/complementary ones.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec2dot2-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
        ```{=html}
        <title>
        ```
        2.2. Study Screening and Selection
        ```{=html}
        </title>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        The inclusion criteria were developed based on the PICO system
        used to form the research question.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        Inclusion criteria were studies that included participants with
        minimum age of 18 years (female or male) with diagnosis of
        substance use disorder (alcohol, opioids, cannabis, and/or
        stimulants). Regarding the intervention, studies that used the
        MBRP as an individual and/or complementary intervention tool
        were included in the present review. As for the comparator, all
        types of interventions were accepted, namely the TAU. As for
        outcomes of interest, we intend to investigate if the MBRP
        interventions are effective in this type of population based on
        indicators supported by other studies in the field
        \[`<xref rid="B9-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}9`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B23-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}23`</xref>`{=html}\].
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        We distinguished the following categories of study designs to be
        included in the present review: experimental designs (randomized
        controlled trials \[RCT\]) and quasi-experimental studies.
        Studies written in English, Spanish, and Portuguese were
        included.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        Exclusion criteria were omission of diagnosis of substance use
        disorder, intervention without recourse to the MBRP, inclusion
        of a population under 18, study design other than experimental
        or quasi-experimental, and studies written in a language other
        than Portuguese, English, or Spanish. The differences found
        between reviewers during this process were resolved through
        dialogue and, when necessary, through a third reviewer.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec2dot3-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
        ```{=html}
        <title>
        ```
        2.3. Data Extraction and Quality Assessment
        ```{=html}
        </title>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        Data were extracted by two independent reviewers, with emphasis
        on specific details related to the specificity of the
        population, the type of co-intervention used, the type of
        comparator, the study design, and relevant results underlining
        the objective and conducting question of the review.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        In order to minimize the risk of bias among the selected
        studies, the Joanna Briggs Institute Checklist for Randomized
        Controlled Trials was used for the critical analysis of the RCT
        included. For the analysis of the quasi-experimental studies,
        the Checklist for Quasi-Experimental Studies was used. To
        examine the methodological quality of the included studies, the
        "Study Appraisal Checklist" was used. This checklist is composed
        of 27 items divided into 5 categories (reporting, external
        validity, bias, confounding, and power) with a higher score
        being equivalent to a higher methodological quality. According
        to the checklist development study
        \[`<xref rid="B24-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}24`</xref>`{=html}\]
        and the other systematic review
        \[`<xref rid="B25-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}25`</xref>`{=html}\]
        using this same instrument, the cut-off point of 14 was used as
        a guideline to define low/good methodological quality of the
        studies.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        The differences found between reviewers during this process were
        resolved through dialogue and, when necessary, through a third
        reviewer. The risks of bias for each type of study included were
        examined in detail (e.g., selection bias). An individual
        assessment of the risk of bias was performed for each of the
        included studies. These results are reported in the
        Methodological Quality section.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec2dot4-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
        ```{=html}
        <title>
        ```
        2.4. Data Analysis
        ```{=html}
        </title>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        The influence of the interventions carried out using the MBRP
        and its effects on the population was described in a narrative
        format for each of the included interventions. A description was
        made taking into account the year in which the studies were
        carried out.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        ```{=html}
        <p>
        ```
        The data analysis was described based on the following topics:
        aim of the study, type of MBRP program used, the researcher
        administrator, type of co-intervention and comparator used, the
        existence of follow-up, and the respective results. Regarding
        the results, they were presented taking into account the
        variables of interest, namely the relapse rate, frequency of
        use, cravings/desire to use, and depression and anxiety
        symptoms.
        ```{=html}
        </p>
        ```
        `</sec>`{=html}`</sec>`{=html}`<sec sec-type="results" id="sec3-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
        ```{=html}
        <title>
        ```
        3.  Results
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            `<sec id="sec3dot1-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            3.1. Search
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            A total of 215 articles were identified as potentially
            relevant to the present review
            (`<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-f001" ref-type="fig">`{=html}Figure
            1`</xref>`{=html}). Of these, 42 were excluded due to being
            duplicate studies. The titles and abstracts of the remaining
            articles were examined taking into account the inclusion and
            exclusion criteria, and 143 more studies were excluded. From
            the 30 studies selected for the full text analysis, 17 were
            excluded for not meeting all the inclusion criteria (seven
            did not mention a diagnosis of substance use disorder, six
            did not have an experimental study design, three did not
            present the outcomes found, and one had a different
            population). Of the 13 studies included, 10 (76.9%) are RCT
            and 3 (23.0%) are quasi-experimental studies without a
            control group.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```

            `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec3dot2-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}

            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            3.2. Characteristics of Included Studies
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            All the studies included were written in English and, as
            previously mentioned, published between 2016 and 2020. We
            decided to include studies since 2016, as some studies
            published this year may not have been considered in the
            previous review. Regarding the country of origin, seven
            studies (53.8.5%) were carried out in the United States
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html}\],
            four (30.7%) in Iran
            \[`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\],
            and two (15.3%) in France
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            The sample sizes ranged from 6 to 191. The type of
            population used was different among the studies: four
            (30.7%) used only individuals with alcohol use disorder
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\],
            three (23.0%) included individuals with substance use
            disorder related to methadone
            \[`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html}\],
            and two (15.3%) used individuals with a stimulant and opioid
            use disorder, respectively
            \[`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html}\].
            On the other hand, three studies (23.0%) had heterogeneous
            samples, including individuals with various substance use
            disorders
            \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            One (7.6%) of the studies did not provide information on the
            characteristics of the sample used
            \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html}\].
            `<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A2" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
            A2`</xref>`{=html} presents the synthesized information of
            the included studies.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec3dot3-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            3.3. Methodological Quality
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            The design of the included studies consisted of 10 (76.9%)
            RCT
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
            and 3 (23.0%) quasi-experimental studies
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            The methodological quality of the studies according to the
            Study Appraisal Checklist varied between 10 and 25 (maximum
            = 28) with an average of 17.3 \[SD = 3.9\]. The studies with
            the lowest and highest quality had values of 37.0% and
            92.5%, respectively. Only two studies were categorized as
            being of low methodological quality
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            A decision was made to include these studies with the goal
            of considering a greater variety of study designs. The
            specific methodological quality flaws that were found in the
            great majority of the included studies are related to the
            distribution of the main confounders in each group
            (`<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}n`</italic>`{=html} = 8), the
            adverse events
            (`<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}n`</italic>`{=html} = 7), the
            representativeness of the sample included
            (`<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}n`</italic>`{=html} = 12),
            the lack of blindness of the assessors
            (`<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}n`</italic>`{=html} = 10),
            and no calculation of the sample power
            (`<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}n`</italic>`{=html} = 9).
            `<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A3" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
            A3`</xref>`{=html} displays the score of each study.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            With regard to the methodological quality of the RCT
            included, the questions (Q2, Q4, Q5, and Q6) of the used
            checklist required refinement in the majority of the
            studies, only being clear in three of them
            (`<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A4" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
            A4`</xref>`{=html}). These questions clarify, respectively,
            whether the allocation to the treatment group was concealed
            (Q2), whether the participants were blind to the assigned
            condition (Q4), whether the researchers who applied the
            intervention were blind to the treatment assignment (Q5),
            and whether the outcome assessors were blind to the
            treatment assignment (Q6).
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            In the study conducted by Glasner et al. (2017;
            \[`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            the allocation into the respective groups was concealed from
            participants but not from the practitioners. On the other
            hand, in the study by Witkiewitz et al. (2019;
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            although both participants and practitioners were blinded to
            the assigned condition, the researchers responsible for
            analyzing the outcomes were not. Regarding the reliability
            of the outcomes and the statistics used, the studies by
            Yaghubi et al. (2017;
            \[`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html}\])
            and Zgierska et al. (2017;
            \[`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html}\])
            were not clear about either the statistical analyses used or
            the outcome measures considered. The study by Zemestani and
            Ottaviani (2016;
            \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\])
            presented greater objectivity and clarity in all the
            procedures performed.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            Regarding the methodological quality of the
            quasi-experimental studies included, seven of the nine
            questions included in the checklist used were clear in all
            studies
            (`<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A5" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
            A5`</xref>`{=html}). These questions referred to the clarity
            of the study with regard to cause and effect (Q1), whether
            the participants were included in any type of comparison
            (Q2), if there was a control group (Q4), if there were
            multiple evaluations of results before and after the
            intervention (Q5), if follow-up was performed (Q6), whether
            the results of the included participants were evaluated in
            the same way (Q7), and whether appropriate statistical
            tests/procedures were used (Q9). In the study of Von
            Hammerstein et al. (2019;
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            it is not clear if the participants received other
            intervention in combination with the intervention of
            interest. In the studies by Zullig et al. (2018;
            \[`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html}\])
            and Biseul et al. (2017;
            \[`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            it is not clear how the reliability of the results measured
            was guaranteed. Neither study presented a control group.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec3dot4-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            3.4. Intervention Characteristics
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            Despite the heterogeneity of the included studies, it was
            found that nine used the traditional 16 h MBRP program
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            The remaining four chose to use a version of the program
            adapted to the population and/or study objective
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\].
            There was also some diversity between studies regarding the
            length of the intervention program and the length of the
            sessions. While the majority of the studies (84.6%) used the
            standard 16 h intervention, with a duration of 120 min for
            each session
            \[`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\],
            two studies chose to adapt the MBRP program according to the
            desired intervention characteristics and/or time available
            for it. One study (7.6%;
            \[`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html}\])
            used the 10 h MBRP program, with each session having a
            duration of 75 min. Another study (7.6%;
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\])
            chose to use a 12 h program, with each session having a
            duration of 90 min.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            At the intervention level, most studies chose to use a
            co-intervention treatment. Three studies (23.1%) used
            medication-assisted treatment (e.g., methadone-therapy) as
            co-intervention
            \[`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html}\],
            with two of those also using it as a comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html}\];
            two (15.3%) used TAU as both co-intervention and comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\];
            one (7.6%) used transcranial direct current stimulation as a
            co-intervention and a sham version of the same intervention
            as a comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\];
            one (7.6%) study chose a contingency management intervention
            that was also a comparator together with a health education
            intervention
            \[`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html}\];
            and one study (7.6%) chose to use cognitive behavior therapy
            as a co-intervention and did not use a comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            The remaining studies did not use any type of
            co-intervention, with one (7.6%) using only TAU as a
            comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\],
            one (7.6%) using a traditional relapse prevention
            intervention
            \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html}\],
            and another (7.6%) using both TAU and relapse prevention
            \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]
            as comparator interventions. Two studies (15.3%) chose not
            to use either a co-intervention or a comparator
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html}\].
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            All but one (7.6%) study
            \[`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html}\]
            used follow-up assessments in order to analyze the
            consistency and reliability of the outcomes obtained.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            `<xref rid="behavsci-11-00133-t0A6" ref-type="table">`{=html}Table
            A6`</xref>`{=html} presents a summary of the results
            described.
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```

            `</sec>`{=html}`<sec id="sec3dot5-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            3.5. Intervention Outcomes
            ```{=html}
            </title>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            Of the 13 included studies, only one did not report
            significant improvements in at least one of the outcome
            measure
            \[`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\].
            The studies by Witkiewitz et al. (2019;
            \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            Roos et al. (2020;
            \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]),
            and Greenfield et al. (2018;
            \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html}\])
            presented a significant decrease in the substance use
            frequency of the participants in the MBRP group: (SE =
            −0.535 (0.16),
            `<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}p`</italic>`{=html} = 0.001),
            (IRR = 0.000003, CI (−0.032, 0.021), and (IRR = 0, 95% CI:
            0,0). Another three studies
            \[`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
            showed a significant reduction in the desire to use
            following the MBRP intervention: (F (2,144) = 35.90,
            `<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}p`</italic>`{=html} \< 0.0001)
            and (F = 374.22,
            `<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}p`</italic>`{=html} = 0.00).
            Moreover, Zemestani and Ottaviani (2016;
            \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\])
            also reported a beneficial impact of MBRP on anxiety (F
            (2,144) = 43.96,
            `<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}p`</italic>`{=html} \< 0.0001)
            and depressive (F (2,144) = 30.73,
            `<italic toggle="yes">`{=html}p`</italic>`{=html} \< 0.0001)
            symptoms. Two other studies found similar results regarding
            the significant improvement of depressive complaints
            \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            ```{=html}
            <p>
            ```
            Lastly, two studies presented significant and positive
            changes on impulsivity levels
            \[`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html}\]
            and quality of life
            \[`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html}\].
            ```{=html}
            </p>
            ```
            `</sec>`{=html}`</sec>`{=html}`<sec sec-type="discussion" id="sec4-behavsci-11-00133">`{=html}
            ```{=html}
            <title>
            ```
            4.  Discussion
                ```{=html}
                </title>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                The objective of the present review was to evaluate the
                effectiveness of MBRP on individuals with SUD. A
                majority of the included studies
                \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\]
                found significant improvements in at least one SUD or
                clinical variable following an MBRP intervention. This
                was true not only for studies that used the original
                MBRP program but also for those that chose a modified
                version (e.g., different program length or session
                duration).
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                Regarding the impact of MBRP on SUD variables, the
                results indicate that participants that received the
                intervention had a significant decrease in frequency of
                use, both measured by days of heavy drinking
                \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]
                and number of drinks per day
                \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\]
                for alcohol and number of using days/substance misuse
                for other substances
                \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
                There was also a significant decrease in cravings/desire
                to use
                \[`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\],
                withdrawal symptoms
                \[`<xref rid="B31-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}31`</xref>`{=html}\],
                and probability of relapse
                \[`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html}\].
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                Concerning other clinical variables, the results suggest
                that participants who received an MBRP intervention saw
                a significant decrease of anxiety
                \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
                and depression
                \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B26-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}26`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\]
                symptoms as well as reduced impulsivity levels
                \[`<xref rid="B32-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}32`</xref>`{=html}\].
                Moreover, they showed a significant improvement in
                quality of life
                \[`<xref rid="B33-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}33`</xref>`{=html}\]
                and coping capacity over time
                \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\].
                These findings seem to be congruent with the subjective
                experience of the participants which, as found by
                Zgierska et al. (2017;
                \[`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html}\]),
                presented an overall satisfaction with the intervention
                and believed it to be helpful for their disorder.
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                Only three studies
                \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
                chose to apply the MBRP program without a
                co-intervention while providing a comparator group. Two
                other studies used TAU as a co-intervention and
                comparator
                \[`<xref rid="B20-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}20`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\].
                This made it difficult to properly compare the
                effectiveness of MBRP against that of other
                evidence-based treatments. Still, the results are
                relevant to understand if new knowledge on the relative
                effectiveness of MBRP has been created since the last
                systematic review conducted by Grant et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B23-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}23`</xref>`{=html}\].
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                Supporting the conclusions from the previous review
                \[`<xref rid="B23-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}23`</xref>`{=html}\],
                Zgierska et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B29-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}29`</xref>`{=html}\]
                found that MBRP plus TAU was not more effective than TAU
                in individuals with alcohol dependence. Even though
                there were no significant group differences, it should
                be noted that individuals from both groups showed
                favorable outcomes. On the other hand, Roos et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]
                and Zemestani and Ottaviani
                \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\],
                who also compared MBRP with TAU, found different
                results. Roos et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]
                reported an interaction by affective symptoms that
                predicted increases in approach coping, which in turn
                led to more favorable substance use related outcomes
                (i.e., fewer heavy drinking days and substance use
                problems at 12 months). The mediator effect of increased
                approach coping was only found for individuals with high
                base-line affective symptoms (as opposed to low or
                moderate). Similarly, Zemestani and Ottaviani
                \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
                found that MBRP was significantly more effective than
                TAU in reducing rates of depression, anxiety, and
                cravings in individuals with SUD and comorbid
                depression. Finally, Greenfield et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B27-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}27`</xref>`{=html}\]
                compared the current most used form of relapse
                prevention
                \[`<xref rid="B8-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}8`</xref>`{=html}\]
                with MBRP and found significant differences regarding
                the decrease of frequency of use.
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                The results from the studies by Roos et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B28-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}28`</xref>`{=html}\]
                and Zemestani and Ottaviani
                \[`<xref rid="B34-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}34`</xref>`{=html}\]
                are particularly interesting. They indicate that MBRP
                may be especially useful for individuals who present SUD
                in comorbidity with severe psychiatric symptomatology,
                namely those with affective disorders. This could be
                explained by the impact mindfulness has been shown to
                have in facilitating emotional regulation. Hölzel et
                al. \[`<xref rid="B36-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}36`</xref>`{=html}\],
                for example, have proposed that the effects of
                mindfulness are a result of changes in mental and brain
                functions pertaining to, among other self-regulatory
                systems, attention and emotional regulation. Similarly,
                Vago and Silbersweig
                \[`<xref rid="B37-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}37`</xref>`{=html}\]
                suggested that changes in self-regulation and
                self-awareness resulting from mindfulness reflect
                modulation in neurocognitive networks associated with
                various dimensions, including emotional regulation. Some
                authors (e.g.,
                \[`<xref rid="B38-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}38`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B39-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}39`</xref>`{=html}\])
                have even argued that mindfulness might employ a unique
                strategy of emotional regulation based on bottom-up
                systems (contrary to cognitive reappraisal, which relies
                on top-down systems). Bottom-up pathways are thought to
                be responsible for emotional processing (generation of
                arousal and affect;
                \[`<xref rid="B40-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}40`</xref>`{=html}\])
                and have been linked to some limbic regions and the
                striatum
                \[`<xref rid="B41-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}41`</xref>`{=html}\].
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                Interestingly, bottom-up along with top-down processes
                have also been hypothesized as possible mediators to the
                effects of mindfulness practice on SUD
                \[`<xref rid="B40-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}40`</xref>`{=html}\].
                This is at least in part explained by the role these and
                associated pathways are proposed to play on the
                reactivity to drug stimuli, drug cravings, and relapse.
                Despite their believed relevance, so far these pathways
                have barely been researched.
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                As with all studies, the present review has limitations
                that need to be considered when interpreting its
                results. It is important to highlight the presence of
                methodological weaknesses in some of the included
                studies, such as a reduced sample size in those using a
                quasi-experimental design
                \[`<xref rid="B22-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}22`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B30-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}30`</xref>`{=html},`<xref rid="B35-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}35`</xref>`{=html}\].
                Given that these weaknesses can skew the results
                obtained, it is not possible generalize them and
                conclude the effectiveness of the intervention under
                study. Another aspect to consider is the significant
                heterogeneity found between studies in relation to the
                main objectives, target population, and type of
                intervention, which invalidated the option of conducting
                a meta-analysis. Beyond the characteristics of the
                studies included, another limitation of this systematic
                review was the reduced number of databases used for the
                article search. A broader search might have led to the
                discovery and inclusion of more studies and, as result,
                a more complete account of the current state of the art
                about this subject matter.
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
                ```{=html}
                <p>
                ```
                In conclusion, we believe the results of the present
                review indicate that MBRP has a significant positive
                impact on various substance use and clinical variables.
                Moreover, even though we could not properly evaluate the
                comparative effectiveness of MBRP in relation to other
                evidence-based treatments, the results suggest that this
                intervention may be of particular value to the treatment
                of individuals with SUD and comorbid psychiatric
                symptomatology. Future studies should not only continue
                to investigate the effectiveness of MBRP in individuals
                with SUD (with and without psychiatric comorbidity) but
                also explore possible mediating variables that may allow
                for a better understanding of it. Furthermore, we
                believe that future research into the effectiveness of
                MBRP would greatly benefit from considering neural
                measures in order to deepen the knowledge of the neural
                circuit mechanisms involved in the practice of
                mindfulness and how these relate to relapse prevention.
                There were few studies that considered neural responses
                in their results, with only one study of interest in
                this area
                \[`<xref rid="B19-behavsci-11-00133" ref-type="bibr">`{=html}19`</xref>`{=html}\].
                ```{=html}
                </p>
                ```
